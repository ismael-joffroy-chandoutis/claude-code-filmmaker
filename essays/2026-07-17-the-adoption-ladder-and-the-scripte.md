# The Adoption Ladder and the Scripte — What an Engineering Maturity Model Misses About Creative Work

*by Ismaël Joffroy Chandoutis — July 2026*

---

On July 16, 2026, Boris Cherny, the creator of Claude Code, published a table called ["Steps of AI Adoption"](https://claude.ai/code/artifact/bfdfaef9-bc62-4dfe-ba9e-c58a26c9accf). Five steps. At step 0 you are gated by your organization. At step 1 you pair with one agent and review every line. At step 2 you orchestrate around ten. At step 3, around a hundred, and the agents start launching each other. At step 4 you steer by intent, a thousand agents deep, and monitor by exception.

I read it the way I read most engineering documents: from the outside, with a translator's suspicion. And what stopped me was a single condition, buried in the transition from step 1 to step 2: *a self-verification loop you trust*. Tests, build, lint, end-to-end checks. The engineer stops reading every line because a machine reads it first. Every rung above that one stands on this loop. Multiply the agents all you want; what actually climbs the ladder is the trust.

My work has no test suite. A cut holds or it doesn't. A scene breathes or it doesn't. There is no command I can run against a rough cut that returns *passing*. When the work is a film, verification is a judgment, and the judgment is the work.

---

## Cinema already solved this, with people

Here is what the table cannot see, and what film crews knew long before software existed: cinema is full of verification loops. They are just made of people.

The scripte (continuity supervisor, in the French tradition) watches every take against every other take, so that the director can look away from continuity and look at the actors. The lab report came back overnight in the photochemical era: scratches, exposure, a hair in the gate, discovered before the set was struck. Dailies existed so that yesterday's work was verified while it could still be reshot. The assistant editor logged and checked every foot of film before the editor touched it.

None of this was bureaucracy. It was trust, engineered. A crew is a structure that lets one person hold a vision while dozens of loops verify the execution. The director who stops checking the gate is standing on a century of accumulated verification, embodied in professions.

Working alone with agents, I lost that inheritance. Solo practice with AI means the crew is gone and the loops went with it. The models write, generate, assemble, answer, at a speed no assistant editor ever matched. And behind them: nothing. No scripte, no lab report, no one who watched the take. An agent will tell you, with perfect confidence, that the work is done. Sometimes the file it describes does not exist.

---

## Building the scripte back, as agents

So the real work of climbing this ladder, for a creative practice, turns out to be the opposite of what I expected. I thought it was orchestration: more sessions, more parallel work, more automation. Orchestration is the easy part, and the table says so too, in its way. The work is rebuilding the scripte.

Concretely, over months, that has meant things like this. An evaluator calibrated against a written grading grid, with thresholds under which work is refused, so that a weak scene gets rejected before I spend an evening on it. One agent whose only power is a veto, checking every generation against the invariants of my visual practice, because an agent that can only say no is the closest thing I have found to a lab report. Panels of adversarial readers, deliberately hostile, applied to anything that leaves the studio, on the theory that a friendly reader verifies nothing. Critical drafts triangulated across three different models, because three statistical machines rarely hallucinate in the same direction. And a hard rule born from failure: an agent claiming to have produced something must show the artifact itself, never its description.

Each of these exists because something went wrong without it. Each is a profession from the old crew, rebuilt as a loop. They are handmade and imperfect, revised after every failure, the way continuity practice itself was refined take by botched take for a century.

---

## The paradox the ladder points at

Step 4 describes someone who steers by intent and monitors by exception. Filmmakers recognize this figure immediately: it is a director's job description. A director never gripped the dolly. Delegating execution while holding a vision is the oldest gesture in this profession.

But there are two ends to hold here, and I want to hold both. Delegation without verification is abandonment; and verification without delegation is the step 1 engineer who never looks away, or the director who checks the gate personally and finishes one film a decade. The whole art, on set as in a terminal, lives between these two, and no rung of any ladder settles it for you. The agents multiplied first in my practice; the trust is still under construction, and honestly it should lag. Trust that arrives before the loop has earned it has a name in both professions: negligence.

So where am I on the five steps? Somewhere between 2 and 3, by the table's own criteria, with routines that wake up before I do and agents that launch agents. And still reading almost everything that matters, like a step 1 beginner. I used to think that gap was a failure of tooling. I now think the gap is the location of the work: an evening spent writing a grading grid for scenes is an evening spent teaching a machine what I check when I check, and I could not have written that grid before trying. The old crew had a century to refine the scripte. I am trying to do it in production, alone, for professions that never existed. It goes wrong regularly. That part, at least, is faithful to film history.
